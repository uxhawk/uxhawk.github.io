UX Design for Android App

Project Directive

In under 100 days, deliver an Android application to the Google Play Store. The application should provide users with a novel engagement model, but also present opportunties to direct traffic back to the company's primary money making application.

My Role

I was the lead UX researcher and designer on this project. I planned all generative research and executed several rounds of evaluative usability testing. Through close collaboration with developers, UI designers, and project managers, I made sure research outcomes had a positive impact on the product roadmap.

Phase I: Ideation

Brainstorming Session

Before launching into any type of user research, the team needed to decide what to build, and we needed to decide fast (the 100 day countdown was already ticking). It was clear we'd start with a minimally viable product (MVP), then ramp up new features post-launch. This app needed to operate in the car insurance world, so it needed to be related to vehicles, insurance, or finances. To generate project ideas, I lead a group ideation session with designers, developers, and project managers.

The goal was to generate as many (judgement free) ideas as possible. I created a set of structured activities to spur creative thinking and help the team land on a project idea. Our ideation session followed the schedule below:

Activity

Duration

Description

One Hand Paper Airplanes

10 Minutes

Make a paper airplane using only one hand. It's a fun way to get started and break people out of their shell.

Flip Assumptions

20 Minutes

Write down as many assumptions as you have about your product. Then, flip those assumptions by listing out the opposite.

Affinity Diagram

30 Minutes

Take the flipped assumptions and rate each on a one-to-seven scale as potential products using the the NUF scale (New, Useful, Feasible).

Ideation Takeaways

Vehicle care emergered as the winner of the NUF analysis. It provided the ideal opportunity to link between existing products in the ecosystem and to develop a stand-alone product with its own benefit to users.

Phase II: Generative Research

User Interviews

We needed to learn a lot about our users before launching into prototyping and design. To build a mental model of our users' beliefs about car ownership, experience with vehicle maintenance, and expectations about app capabilities, I recruited 12 users to participate in semi-structured user interviews. The three participant groups (four per group) were:

  1. Owned their first car for less than one year

  2. Owned vehicles for two to five years

  3. Owned vehicles for more than five years

Sampling users from high, medium, and low car owning experience helped me understand how knowledge, expectations, and behavior changes as people become more accustomed to owning a vehicle.

Sample Interview Questions

Q1

What stands out to you about driving off the lot with your new car?

Q2

What do you know now that you wish you knew before you bought your car?

Q3

What resources have you used to help you deal with issues that have come up with your car?

Q4

Have their been any situations where you've felt overwhelmed or underprepared?

Interview Takeaways

  • Car owners across groups have a range of vehicle knowledge (high, medium, low) and varying degrees of willingness to work on their own cars.

  • Electronic manuals are more helpful to new car owners while experienced car owners feel equally comfortable using either an electronic or glove box manual.

  • People feel very attached to their vehicles across all participant groups.

Personas From Generative Research

The insights from the user interviews and their survey responses led to the creation of a persona named Sue. Sue's uncertainty during the onboarding experience, lack of confidence in her insurance, and difficulty adopting technology became key problems to solve.

Existing Customer Survey

In addition to the interviews, I distributed a survey to existing customers to compile data on the perceptions of active users. This helped me create a well-rounded model of what it meant to be a "new customer."

Sample Survey Questions

Q1

I was confident in my knowledge about insurance.

Q2

I had everything I needed as a policy holder.

Q3

I needed the help of technical person to use the app.

Q4

I would like to use this app frequently.

Responses to the survey indicated room for improvement in making the existing onboarding experience more educational and less tech-savvy users.

Phase II: Evaluative Research

Usability Testing

The usability testing goals were to establish a baseline of user performance and identify potential design concerns to be addressed in order to improve the efficiency, productivity, and end-user satisfaction. The Usability Test Plan Template provided a scaffolding to structure research efforts.

View Testing Objectives

  • Identify design inconsistencies and usability problem areas within the user interface and content areas. Potential sources of error may include:

    • Navigation errors – failure to locate functions, excessive keystrokes to complete a function, failure to follow recommended task flows.

    • Presentation errors – failure to locate and properly act upon desired information in screens, selection errors due to labeling ambiguities.

    • Control usage problems – improper interactions or incorrect usage of UI components.

  • Exercise the application under controlled test conditions with representative users to assess whether usability goals regarding an effective, efficient, and well-received user interface have been achieved.

  • Establish baseline user performance and user-satisfaction levels of the user interface for future usability evaluations.

Methods

Data were collected on this project through remote, unmoderated usability tests. This allowed for rapid data collection from target users across the country. Participants were recruited and paid through usertesting.com. Sessions lasted approximately 40 minutes during which time users practiced think aloud protocols and shared their screens to record interactions.

Scenarios

Users completed the following tasks during the usability test. The questions gave users the opportunity to interact with a variety of functions within the application without focusing on one area for too long.

Tasks

Q1

Imagine you just bought car insurance and downloaded the company's app. Log in to pay your bill.

Q2

You were about to head to work in the morning but left your headlights on all night. Your battery is dead and you need assistance. Show what you would do to get help.

Q3

What stood out about the app permission screens you saw after you signed into the application?

Q4

What are your thoughts about the language used to grant app permissions you saw after you signed in?

Satisfaction Questionnaire

Users responded to a series of Likert scale questions to share their perceived difficulty ratings, overall satisfaction, and confidence during their interactions. The Nielsen Norman Group's guide to measuring perceived usability was a great survey design resource.

Post-test Questions

Q1

I thought there was too much inconsistency in this system.

Q2

I had to work hard to complete each of my tasks.

Q3

Overall, the app was easy to use.

Q4

I was able to successfully accomplish what I was asked to do.

Results from the satisfaction questionnaire indicated that modifying the onboarding experience (by adding slides to the permissions granting process) increased the perceived usability of the application.

Design Iterations

Through several rounds of usability testing with low-fidelity prototypes, I identified the ideal length for a new onboarding experience by presenting study groups with experiences of varying lengths. Users' response to a satisfaction question identified the three slide option as the best choice.

Having users interaction with prototypes of the the entire account creation process helped create a realistic scenarios by which I could judge fatigue and willingness to engage with new content.

Final Designs

Benchmark Comparisons

Using the final designs, I asked users the same survey questions that I sent to existing customers at the start of the project. Comparing average scores indicated the new designs should have a positive impact on the onboarding experience and help new customers engage in self-management of their policies.
