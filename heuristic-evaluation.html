Air Canada Heuristic Evaluation

Project Directive

Assess the usability of Air Canada's mobile application and create a customer journey map for customers:

  • Shopping for flights

  • Tracking flight of friends and family members

The Nielsen Norman Group's 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design served as the basis for the analysis of the application's usability. Understanding the strength's and shortcomings of common task flows within the application are essential before iterating through wireframes, prototypes, and high fidelity designs.

Booking a Trip

The first screens I evaluated were users that needed to book a flight.

Heuristic

Observation

1

Efficiency of Use

A flight search takes​ numerous variables into account. The​ “Recents” link allows users to quickly recall complex previous searches.

2

Visibility of System Status

Segmented controls make it difficult to identify current selection. Does black or white mean it’s selected?

3

Understandable Interface Objects

Icons in form fields quickly and accurately relay significance.​

4

Error Prevention

Users should not be able to select invalid starting points and destinations.​

Selecting Specific Flights

Next, I reviewed the pages in the task flow where users chose departing and returning flights.

Heuristic

Observation

1

User control and freedom

Flight searches often have hundreds of results. Narrowing results to specific requirements expedites decision making.

2

Recover from Errors / Flexibility

It’s easy for users to see if they chose the wrong date and identify which departure dates are cheapest.​

3

Discoverability

What is the difference between Flex and Latitude? Any attempt to hide complexity will serve to increase it.​

Customer Journey Map

After reviewing the basic transactions within Air Canada's travel app, I had a good understanding of how a user would feel throughout their shopping and travel experience. I created a customer journey map that closely considered pain points from the heuristic evaluation.
